The morning air in Itabashi had a sharp, clean bite to it as I stepped off the train. I’ll be honest: I actually missed the correct exit at Motohasunuma Station, distracted by a particularly weathered vending machine I wanted to photograph. But that’s the beauty of wandering with a camera—getting lost usually leads you exactly where you need to be. Following a narrow side street, the sounds of the city began to fade, replaced by the rhythmic *crunch-crunch* of my boots on gravel. That’s when I saw it: the stone torii gate of Hasunuma Hikawa Shrine, standing like a silent sentry between the modern apartments.

### 1. 🙏 Deities and Origins: The Guardian of the Old Village

Stepping through the gate, I felt the atmosphere shift. It’s a "Chinju"—a local guardian—and you can feel that protective energy immediately. I stood for a moment, letting my eyes adjust to the dappled sunlight filtering through the trees. This place has been watching over the neighborhood for centuries, long before the concrete took over. 

I learned that the name *Hasunuma* literally means "Lotus Pond." Back in the day, this whole area was a landscape of wetlands and quiet water. The villagers enshrined **Susanoo-no-Mikoto (素戔嗚尊)** here, praying for protection against the floods that used to sweep through the marshes. Standing by the wooden hall, I could almost smell the ghost of those ancient lotuses beneath the scent of aged cedar and incense. Even though the ponds are gone, Susanoo-no-Mikoto still stands guard, offering *Yakuyoke* (protection from misfortune) to anyone who takes a moment to bow.

***

### 2. ⛩️ A Stroll Through the Precincts: A Personal Photo Diary

The beauty of Hasunuma Hikawa Shrine is how utterly unpretentious it is. There are no tour buses, no megaphones—just the rustle of the wind in the branches. I spent a good twenty minutes just waiting for the light to hit the texture of the weathered wood on the main hall. I watched an elderly man in a salt-and-pepper beret stop by, offer a quick prayer, and continue his walk home. It felt like I was witnessing a private conversation between the neighborhood and its gods.

### 3. 📜 Goshuin and Shrine Items: Simple Blessings

I wandered over to the *Shamusho* (shrine office), but the window was closed—a common occurrence at these quiet, local spots during the week. It didn't bother me; it just added to the feeling that this place isn't "performing" for anyone. If you visit during the New Year or a festival day, you can get a beautiful hand-stamped *Goshuin*. For now, I just looked at the *Omamori* (amulets) on display through the glass. They focus on *Kanai Anzen*—the safety of the family. It’s a humble, lovely sentiment that perfectly fits this neighborhood’s soul.

***

### 4. 🌃 What to See Nearby: Shimizuzaka Park

After soaking in the silence of the shrine, I needed to stretch my legs, so I headed over to **Shimizuzaka Park**. It’s just a short walk toward Kita-ku, and honestly, it’s a hidden gem. The park is built into a hill, and there’s this incredible artificial mountain stream that sounds like a real forest creek. 

But the highlight? The 52-meter roller slide. I’ll admit, I hesitated for about three seconds before deciding that "solo traveler" also means "nobody I know is watching," and I went for it. It’s surprisingly fast and, frankly, the most fun I’ve had all week. If you need a dose of nature (and a bit of childish joy), don’t skip this.

### ✨ Editor's Closing Remarks

Hasunuma Hikawa Shrine is exactly why I travel. It’s not a "must-see" on a Top 10 list, but it’s a "must-feel" if you want to understand the heartbeat of Tokyo’s residential life. I ended my visit sitting on a wooden bench, watching the shadows grow longer across the courtyard, just breathing. If you find yourself on the Toei Mita Line, get off at Motohasunuma. Turn off your GPS for a bit. Let the "Lotus Pond" find you. It’s the perfect, quiet antidote to the neon chaos of the city center.
